The brief

A Cape Town streetwear label that needed to look the part.

Void CPT is a concept streetwear brand rooted in Cape Town culture — limited drops, no restocks, raw identity. The brief was to build a site that felt as intentional as the clothes: dark, editorial, and built for a generation that can spot a template from a mile away.

The challenge wasn't making it look cool. The challenge was making it convert — getting visitors to act before a drop sells out — without losing the aesthetic that makes the brand worth caring about in the first place.

What they needed

A full storefront with product drops, category navigation, brand story, and newsletter sign-up for drop alerts. Mobile-first, fast-loading, no compromise on design.

What they didn't want

Templates. Generic e-commerce layouts. Anything that looked like it could belong to any other brand. The site had to be unmistakably Void CPT.

The audience

18–30 year olds in Cape Town and SA's urban centres. Culturally sharp, visually literate, loyal to brands that feel authentic and locally rooted.

The constraint

Pure HTML, CSS, and JS — no frameworks, no CMS. The client needed to own the code outright and keep hosting costs at zero.

Design decisions

Every choice had a reason.

Good design isn't decoration. Every decision here was made in service of the brand and the user.

01

Void black over standard dark

The background is #050505 — not dark grey, not navy. True void. It makes every element on the page feel like it's floating. For a brand called Void CPT, this wasn't a stylistic choice, it was the only honest one.

02

The scrolling ticker at the hero base

Drop alerts, shipping info, scarcity signals — the ticker communicates all three without taking up any real estate. It keeps the hero clean while doing genuine conversion work underneath. Borrowed from streetwear brand sites that actually sell.

03

Product cards as full-bleed image containers

The product grid uses aspect-ratio-locked cards with overlaid text at the bottom. No separate product title rows, no cluttered metadata. The image does the selling; the overlay handles the essentials. Tap to explore — not tap to find out what it is.

04

The manifesto section between products and shop

"We didn't come from fashion week. We came from the Cape Flats." This sits between product sections — not at the bottom where no one reads it. Culture-first brands need their story in the scroll path, not buried. It reframes the purchase as something worth making.

05

Crosshair cursor

A small detail that lands hard for the right audience. The crosshair cursor signals precision, edge, and intentionality — three things the brand stands for. It costs nothing and communicates volumes to anyone paying attention.
